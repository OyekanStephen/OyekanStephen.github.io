drowning Jupiter

Unraveling the simplicity of our solar system

thought experiment:


if we one day study and understand gravity enough to be able to build a black hole in our solar system.
What will be the size of the black hole we will get from the gas giant planet in our solar system?
What will happen to the 95 moons orbiting Jupiter, will they just spiral into the black hole adding to its already massive mass?
Will it drastically influence the orbits of nearby planets?

JUPITER'S EVENT HORIZON

Let's start this complex and challenging experiment with a little context of what we are working with. Let's calculate its Schwarzschild radius. This is the formula by Karl that lets us calculate how small a mass needs to be to have its escape velocity equal to the speed of light thus an event horizon.

Formula:
Rs = 2GM/c² - G = Gravitational constant (6.6743 × 10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²)
- M = Mass of the object (in this case, Jupiter's mass, 1.898 × 10²⁷ kg)
- c = Speed of light (2.9979 × 10⁸ m/s)
Plugging in the values, we get:
Rs = 2 * (6.6743 × 10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²) * (1.898 × 10²⁷ kg) / (2.9979 × 10⁸ m/s)²
Rs ≈ 2.82 meters

Therefore, the Schwarzschild radius of Jupiter is approximately 2.82 meters.
We will get a black hole 5.64 meters wide black hole, that's like a car-sized tunnel.
Will the immense and concentrated gravitational force impact the dynamic Jupiter's moons?

IMPACT ON OUR SOLAR SYSTEM

Surprisingly, If Jupiter were to be turned into a black hole, the orbits of its moons would remain largely unchanged. Here's why:

According to Kepler's third law, the square of a moon's orbital period (T) is proportional to the cube of its semi-major axis (a) and inversely proportional to the total mass (M) of the system:

T^2 = (4π^2 / G) * a^3 / M

Since both G and M remain the same when Jupiter is replaced with a black hole, the orbital period (T) of each moon wouldn't change.
Also, the law of planetary motion dictates that the orbital parameters of a celestial body depend solely on its mass and the mass of the central body it orbits. These laws are independent of the composition or type of the central body, as long as its mass remains the same.
Therefore, in making a black hole the mass of Jupiter wouldn't alter the gravitational constant (G) or the total mass of the Jupiter-moon system. One noticeable change that might be observed is the change in the tidal forces, which depend on the central body's size and rotation, which might be slightly different from a black hole due to its extreme compactness and change of rotation.

impacts on jupiter moons

If Jupiter was turned into a black hole with the same mass, the immediate orbits of the other planets would not significantly change. This might seem surprising, but it's due to the nature of gravity and mass.

Here's why:

Gravity depends on mass, not size or density, The gravitational pull a celestial body exerts on another is determined by its mass, not its size or density. Since the replacement black hole would have the same mass as Jupiter, its gravitational pull on the other planets would remain essentially the same.

Orbits depend on the relative mass and distance: Planetary orbits are determined by the balance between the Sun's gravitational pull and the planet's inertia. In our solar system, the Sun's mass dominates everything else, meaning its gravitational pull primarily dictates the planets' orbital radii. Jupiter's influence, while significant, is much smaller in comparison.

To quantify this, let's use Newton's law of universal gravitation:

F = G( M1 x M2)/ r²

where:
F is the gravitational force
G is the gravitational constant
M1 and M2 are the masses of the two objects
r is the distance between their centers

Applying this equation to the Sun and Earth before and after replacing Jupiter with a black hole, we see that the force (and hence the acceleration) remains nearly the same, causing insignificant changes in Earth's orbital radius. This applies to other planets as well.
However, there might be some notable impact:

Slightly altered orbital shapes: While the average distances might remain largely unchanged, the orbits could become slightly more elliptical over time due to interactions with other planets and asteroids.

Increased asteroid impacts:
Without Jupiter's gravitational "shepherding" role, the influx of asteroids and comets towards the inner planets might increase slightly.

Replacing Jupiter with a black hole of the same mass wouldn't drastically alter the planetary orbits. Our solar system would continue its cosmic dance but with a much darker resident in place of the giant gas giant.

THE PLANETARY BALANCE

The journey, initially sparked by the curiosity of substituting Jupiter with a black hole, turned into a realization of the elegant simplicity underlying celestial mechanics. While my initial excitement about this project was in the prospect of delving into planetary simulations and solving difficult mathematical physics problems, the outcome revealed the resilience of planetary orbits, showing the remarkable stability of our solar system. This thought experiment serves as a reminder that in the vast complexities, adopting a simpler perspective can foster a profound appreciation for the harmonious choreography of celestial bodies and the foundational principles dictating their movements. The unforeseen revelation prompts contemplation on the idea that, within the cosmic wonders, a nuanced balance between complexity and simplicity offers a valuable lens for unraveling the universe's mysteries.

Now, the question beckons: Even if no negative impact on our solar system, Shall we embark on this endeavor? Are we prepared to push the boundaries of science to their utmost limits, even if it entails sacrificing a celestial king in the process? The answer, my friend, resonates as a timeless echo in my heart.
